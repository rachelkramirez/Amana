Why this guide exists

Most mothers do not realize
what they could have asked.

Most mothers do not know they had the right to ask different questions, push back on a recommendation, or change providers until they are already postpartum. By the time they piece together what happened in that birth room, the experience is behind them.

This guide exists to change that. Knowing your rights and knowing what to look for before you are in a vulnerable moment changes outcomes. The research on birth support backs this up, and so does the experience of every mother who walked into a birth room prepared.

I have been in birth spaces where a mother made a clear, informed decision and the nursing staff spent her waiting room time trying to pressure her out of it. I have seen providers transfer their personal anxiety onto a laboring woman. I have watched providers continue educating a mother about an intervention she already declined, just in case she changed her mind.

You have the right to build a team that actually works for you. This guide will help you do that.

Your OB, midwife, and nurses answer to a hospital, a practice, and a protocol. Your doula answers only to you. But even without doula support, every person on your birth team should respect your autonomy in every room you walk into.
Know this first

Your rights in any
birth setting.

Before you evaluate anyone on your team, know what you are entitled to. These are established legal and ethical standards in healthcare, grounded in the principle of patient autonomy.

Informed Consent
Every intervention requires your informed, voluntary agreement. You must be told what it is, why it is being recommended, the alternatives, and what happens if you decline. Silence or nodding along does not constitute consent.
The Right to Decline
You can decline any recommended intervention at any time, including during active labor. A provider who continues pushing after a clear decline is acting outside the bounds of ethical care.
The Right to Ask Questions
Ask why, what the alternatives are, what the risks of waiting are, and what evidence supports the recommendation. The BRAIN framework covers this: Benefits, Risks, Alternatives, Intuition, Nothing (what happens if we do nothing).
Freedom from Coercion
Fear-based language used to pressure you into a decision is a form of coercion. You have the right to make decisions based on complete, balanced information without emotional pressure applied to the outcome.
The Right to Change Providers
You can change your OB, midwife, or birth setting at any point in pregnancy, including the third trimester. You are never locked in to a provider who does not respect your choices.
A Support Person
You have the right to have a continuous support person present, including a doula, in most birth settings. Confirm your facility's policy in advance and get any restrictions in writing.
What to watch for

Red flags and green flags
in your birth team.

These patterns are real and documented across maternal health research. Recognizing them before you are in a vulnerable moment is one of the most practical things you can do to protect your birth experience.

Red Flags
Nursing staff use fear-based language to pressure you out of a decision you have already made, particularly in a waiting room or without your provider present. You are allowed to say: "I have already made my decision and I do not want to continue this conversation." You can also ask to speak with your provider directly.
Your provider expresses personal fear or anxiety about your birth choices in a way that appears designed to change your decision rather than inform it. A provider's discomfort with your informed choice belongs to them. You can acknowledge their concern and restate your decision clearly: "I hear your concern. My decision stands."
You decline an intervention clearly and your provider returns to the topic repeatedly across multiple appointments or during labor. One clear explanation of their recommendation is appropriate. Returning to a declined intervention without new clinical information crosses into pressure. Document the date and what was said.
Your questions are minimized, dismissed, or met with "that's just how we do things here" or "you'll understand when the time comes." You deserve a provider who can explain their reasoning every time you ask. Vague or dismissive answers in the office predict the same behavior in the birth room.
Your birth preferences are met with eye rolls, dismissiveness, or comments suggesting they will be irrelevant once labor begins. How a provider responds to your birth plan before labor is a direct preview of how they will respond to your requests during labor.
You feel rushed, unheard, or like your visit is an inconvenience at multiple appointments. Research consistently shows that provider communication quality and patient trust are among the strongest predictors of birth satisfaction and outcomes. A pattern of feeling dismissed is worth acting on.
Green Flags
Your provider presents recommendations with a clear explanation of the evidence behind them, including the option of waiting or declining, and moves forward with your decision without returning to it.
Your questions are welcomed across every appointment. Your provider makes time, does not rush you, and invites you to come back with more.
When you share your birth preferences, your provider engages with them specifically, noting where they can support your plan and being honest about any clinical constraints without dismissing your wishes.
Your partner or support person is acknowledged, included in conversations, and given clear information about how to advocate for you during labor.
You leave appointments feeling genuinely informed and steady. A provider who respects your intelligence gives you what you need to make your own decisions rather than making decisions for you.
If you are already seeing red flags
  • Document specific incidents with dates, what was said, and who was present. This creates a record if you need to escalate.
  • Request a formal meeting with your provider to discuss your concerns directly before labor begins.
  • Ask whether another provider in the practice could take over your care, or whether a transfer of care is possible.
  • Bring a support person to every appointment who can witness conversations and advocate alongside you.
  • Consult a doula even if you have not hired one yet. Many doulas offer single consultations to help you navigate these situations.
  • Know that changing providers, even late in pregnancy, is your right. It has been done and it can be done safely.

Before you commit

Questions to ask every
person on your birth team.

How a provider responds to direct questions tells you more than the answers themselves. A provider who becomes defensive, vague, or dismissive when asked these questions in a consultation is showing you who they will be in the room.

For your OB or midwife
What is your c-section rate, and what are the most common reasons you recommend one?
A provider who answers this openly is standing behind their practice. Ask for a number, not a philosophy.
For your OB or midwife
If I decline a recommended intervention, how do you handle that in the moment and in my chart?
This tells you whether they treat your refusal as a clinical decision to be documented or an obstacle to be overcome.
For your OB or midwife
Who will actually be at my birth? Will it be you, or whoever is on call that day?
Many women build trust with one provider and meet a stranger in the birth room. Know this in advance.
For your OB or midwife
How do you approach routine interventions like continuous fetal monitoring, IV placement, or time limits on pushing?
Understanding their default protocols helps you prepare for the conversations you may need to have.
For your doula
How do you support a client when her birth plan changes significantly from what she originally wanted?
A doula who follows your lead in any birth circumstance is different from one who is invested in a particular type of birth.
For your doula
How do you handle tension between what I want and what my medical team is recommending in real time?
You need to know she will hold your voice clearly without creating conflict that could compromise your care.
For your doula
What does your prenatal preparation actually look like, and how many visits are included before my due date?
A doula who does not invest in your preparation before labor cannot show up fully informed when labor begins.
For your doula
Have you supported Black mothers specifically, and how do you approach the unique context of birthing as a Black woman?
Cultural humility and awareness of racial disparities in maternal care are not optional for a doula serving Black families.
For any provider
Can you walk me through what informed consent looks like in your practice, specifically?
If they cannot describe their process clearly, they may not be practicing it consistently.
For your birth setting
What are your policies on continuous fetal monitoring, freedom of movement, eating and drinking, and pushing positions?
Facility policies can significantly shape your birth experience regardless of your provider preferences.

Why doula support
changes the math.

The Cochrane Review, the gold standard of medical evidence synthesis, analyzed over 26 studies on continuous labor support. Mothers with a doula present were significantly less likely to have a cesarean birth, less likely to use pain medication, and more likely to report a positive birth experience compared to those without continuous support.

The key word is continuous. A nurse who is covering multiple rooms, a midwife who arrives at transition, a partner doing their best without training: none of these provide continuous, informed, unwavering support the way a doula does. Your doula is the one person in the room whose only job is you.

Finally

Your instincts are
data too.

Every question in this guide matters. But so does the feeling in your body when you leave an appointment. Pay attention to whether you feel smaller after a visit than when you walked in. Notice whether you are rehearsing how to say things so your provider will not push back, or leaving out parts of your birth plan to avoid conflict. That discomfort is worth taking seriously.

You are allowed to change providers, interview multiple doulas, and ask the same question more than once until you get a clear answer. The right team will make you feel informed, respected, and steady before labor begins, which is exactly when it matters.

Using the BRAIN framework in the birth room
  • B   Benefits: What are the potential benefits of this intervention for me and my baby?
  • R   Risks: What are the potential risks or downsides?
  • A   Alternatives: What other options exist, including less invasive ones?
  • I   Intuition: What is my gut telling me about this decision?
  • N   Nothing: What happens if we wait, do nothing, or revisit this in an hour?

You are allowed to use this framework in any birth setting. Writing it on an index card and bringing it to the hospital is something many mothers have done. Your doula can help you use it in real time when you are in labor and your capacity to advocate for yourself may be stretched.
